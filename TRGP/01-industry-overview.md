# Midstream Natural Gas Infrastructure Industry Overview

**Prepared for:** TRGP (Targa Resources Corp.) Equity Analysis
**Date:** March 24, 2026

---

## 1. Industry Purpose and Core Economics

The midstream natural gas and NGL (natural gas liquids) infrastructure industry exists to move, process, and transform hydrocarbons from wellhead to end-market. It sits between upstream producers (who drill and extract) and downstream consumers (refiners, petrochemical plants, exporters, and end users).

The core value proposition: raw natural gas from a well contains impurities and mixed NGLs that must be removed and separated before the gas and NGL products can be sold. Midstream operators provide the physical infrastructure -- gathering pipelines, processing plants, fractionation facilities, transportation pipelines, storage terminals, and export docks.

Revenue is generated primarily through: (1) Fee-based contracts tied to throughput volumes, (2) Commodity-based margins from purchasing raw gas, extracting NGLs, and selling the components, and (3) Marketing margins from optimizing the timing and location of commodity sales.

The economic model resembles a toll road: once infrastructure is built, it generates recurring cash flows from volumes flowing through the system with relatively low marginal operating costs.

## 2. Industry Structure and Competitive Shape

The U.S. midstream sector is dominated by a handful of large infrastructure operators. Key players include Targa Resources (TRGP), Enterprise Products Partners (EPD), Williams Companies (WMB), ONEOK (OKE), Energy Transfer (ET), Kinder Morgan (KMI), and MPLX.

Competition is regional and asset-specific. Once a gathering system is connected to a producer's wells, switching costs are extremely high. This creates local monopoly dynamics in gathering. Fractionation and transportation are more competitive because multiple operators can access the same NGL hub (Mont Belvieu being the dominant U.S. NGL market center).

## 3. Revenue and Profitability Drivers

Volume growth is the primary driver. The Permian Basin has been the dominant growth engine since ~2017. Contract structure determines commodity price risk exposure: fee-based (pure volume), percent-of-proceeds, or keep-whole arrangements. The industry trend has been toward higher fee-based percentages.

NGL pricing is a secondary but significant driver, influenced by petrochemical demand, heating fuel demand, and export demand. Profitability is measured primarily through Adjusted EBITDA and operating margin per unit of throughput.

## 4. Supply Side, Cost Structure, and Constraints

Capital intensity is the defining constraint. A 275 MMcf/d gas processing plant costs hundreds of millions. A 500-mile NGL pipeline costs billions. These investments must be made 2-3 years before volumes materialize. Producer activity dependence is critical -- midstream operators do not control drilling decisions.

## 5. Technology, Regulation, and Structural Change

LPG/NGL exports have been transformative. The U.S. became the largest LPG exporter globally. Methane emissions regulation is tightening. Natural gas is positioned as a transition fuel with growing demand from LNG exports, power generation, and data centers.

## 6. Medium-Term Outlook (3-10 Years)

The Permian Basin should remain the primary growth driver through at least 2030. NGL demand growth is supported by petrochemical capacity and LPG export expansion. Capital discipline has improved across the sector.

## KEY META: Investor Synthesis

1. Core economic engine: Toll-road economics on essential hydrocarbon infrastructure.
2. Primary growth lever: Permian Basin production growth and NGL export demand.
3. Most underappreciated constraint: Capital intensity of growth requiring billions in upfront investment.
4. Key risk: Sustained oil price downturn slowing Permian drilling combined with NGL oversupply.
5. What compounders look for: Integrated systems, high fee-based percentages, scale advantages, capital discipline.
