# TRGP Bear Case: The Munger Invert
## How Could I Lose Money?

**Date:** March 24, 2026
**Source:** FY2025 10-K (filed February 19, 2026), Finnhub, Alpha Vantage

---

### 1. What is the most likely way an investor could lose money here?

Valuation compression. At 27.9x trailing earnings, 16.6x price-to-book, and 14.1x EV/EBITDA, TRGP is priced for continued high growth. If Permian production growth slows -- due to lower oil prices, regulatory changes, or natural basin maturation -- the stock could re-rate to a lower multiple while earnings growth stalls. A reversion to even 11-12x EV/EBITDA (still reasonable for midstream) would imply 15-20% downside from current levels.

The second most likely scenario: the massive capital program ($3.3B in growth capex in 2025, likely similar in 2026-2027) delivers returns below the cost of capital due to NGL oversupply, construction delays, or cost inflation.

### 2. Where is the business structurally weak?

**Commodity exposure remains meaningful.** Despite the shift toward fee-based contracts, Targa still has significant commodity price sensitivity. The 2025 10-K shows:
- NGL prices fell 9% year-over-year
- Condensate prices fell 10%
- Natural gas prices rose 75% but from a very low base
- The "Other" segment swung from -$164.6M to -$5.3M purely on mark-to-market derivative changes

**Producer concentration in the Permian.** Total Permian volumes represent 80% of Targa's plant natural gas inlet (6,391 of 8,016 MMcf/d). If the Permian disappoints, there is no meaningful diversification to fall back on. Central volumes declined 2%, Badlands declined 4%, and Coastal declined 2% in 2025.

**Customer concentration risk.** The gathering business depends on a relatively small number of large E&P companies. Consolidation in the E&P sector gives producers more bargaining power on contract renegotiations.

### 3. What assumptions need to go right -- on edge or narrow odds?

For the stock to justify its current valuation:
- Permian production must continue growing 8-11% annually for the next 3+ years
- All 10 processing plants under construction or ordered must come online on time and on budget
- The Speedway NGL pipeline (500 miles, 30-inch, expected 3Q 2027) must be completed without significant overruns
- NGL demand must grow fast enough to absorb the massive increase in fractionation capacity (Trains 11, 12, 13 adding 450 MBbl/d)
- Interest rates must not spike further, given $852.8M in annual interest expense
- Commodity prices must remain in a range that supports both producer drilling and Targa's margins

This is a lot of things that all need to go right simultaneously.

### 4. What could permanently impair earnings or cash flow?

- **Permian Basin production plateau or decline.** The natural decline rate of shale wells means the Permian needs massive continued drilling just to maintain production. If drilling slows, volumes decline rapidly.
- **Regulatory action against NGL exports.** Export bans or restrictions (politically possible in certain scenarios) would crush NGL prices and Targa's export terminal value.
- **Stranded assets.** If the energy transition accelerates faster than expected, midstream infrastructure built today with 30-40 year useful lives could face impairment.
- **A severe and prolonged commodity downturn.** Targa survived the 2020 crash, but another sustained downturn while carrying a heavy capital program would stress the balance sheet.

### 5. Is the valuation pricing in perfection?

Essentially, yes. At $237/share:
- P/E of 27.9x is high for a midstream company (peers typically trade at 10-18x)
- Price-to-book of 16.6x suggests the market values Targa's assets far above their accounting value
- EV/EBITDA of 14.1x is at the upper end of midstream range
- Dividend yield of 1.67% is well below the midstream average of 4-7%

The market is pricing TRGP as a growth stock, not a yield vehicle. If growth disappoints, the re-rating could be severe because there is no yield floor to support the stock.

### 6. Where could management hurt shareholders?

**Capital allocation risk.** Management is spending aggressively: $3.3B in growth capex in 2025, with a pipeline of even more projects. Each new plant and pipeline is a bet on future volumes. If management over-builds ahead of demand, returns on invested capital will decline.

**Acquisition risk.** The Blackcomb Joint Venture and Stakeholder Acquisition (funded with $1.25B in borrowings in January 2026) show management is willing to do large deals. Overpaying for acquisitions is a perennial risk.

**Dilution.** Equity compensation of $69.5M in 2025. While modest relative to earnings, this is a steady drag.

### 7. Why might investors be fooling themselves?

- **Recency bias.** The Permian has been a one-way growth story for 8+ years. Investors may be extrapolating recent growth rates indefinitely. All basins eventually plateau.
- **Confusing revenue growth with value creation.** Revenue grew only 4% while growth capex was $3.3B. The return on incremental capital is the real question.
- **Adjusted free cash flow is thin.** After all capex, TRGP generated only $539M in adjusted free cash flow on $17B in revenue. That is a 3.2% FCF margin. The dividend alone costs approximately $860M, meaning it is being partially funded by debt/revolver.
- **The "toll road" metaphor overstates predictability.** Targa has real commodity price exposure and real volume risk. It is not a pure toll road.

### 8. What evidence would prove this bear case right?

- Permian rig count declining below 250 rigs for more than two consecutive quarters
- NGL prices (particularly propane and butane) declining 20%+ from current levels
- Processing plant utilization rates falling below 80%
- Leverage ratio exceeding 4.0x Debt/EBITDA
- Dividend coverage ratio falling below 1.0x
- Management announcing delays or cost overruns on Speedway, Trains 11-13, or the LPG export expansion
- Oil prices sustaining below $55/bbl for 6+ months
